I am a graduate student in the Schumer lab at Stanford University. I'm interested in how hybridization in swordtail fishes fascilitates adaptation to environmental stress, like elevated water temperatures, in parental species and hybrid populations. Previously, I was a bioinformatics technician in the Palumbi lab at Hopkins Marine Station (HMS).

I received my B.S. in marine biology and computer science from UC San Diego. As an undergraduate I studied evolution and bioluminescence in marine invertebrates at Scripps Institution of Oceanography (SIO) in the Rouse and Deheyn labs, and developed phylogenetics tools at the San Diego Supercomputer Center (SDSC).

Past Projects

  • Finding genes involved in conferring thermal tolerance in the reef-building coral Acropora hyacinthus
    Palumbi Lab @ Stanford HMS

    Some A. hyacinthus coral populations are more resilient to heat stress than others. Using comparative genomics and RNA-seq methods, we identified variation between individuals from these populations that likely contributes to thermal tolerance. This work has the potential to inform reef restoration and management efforts, by providing markers for identifying "strong" corals that will be best-equipped to survive increasing ocean temperatures.

    Visit the project page and repo for more info.

  • Development of multiplex PCR pipeline for efficient Queen Conch genetic profiling
    Palumbi Lab @ Stanford HMS

    Several Queen Conch populations in the Caribbean are CITES-listed, meaning they are (or recommended to be) protected against over-exploitation by an international trade agreement. A new technique employing multiplex PCR allows the mass amplification and sequencing of genetic markers from individual conch in the trade, which can be used to determine their population origins. This method will be useful for Queen Conch fishery management, and can be easily applied to nonmodel organisms beyond conch.

    Visit the project repo.
